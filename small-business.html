Small Business Owners

Financial Planning for Small Business Owners

Running a business is hard enough. Your financial plan shouldn't add to the stress.

Small business owners are some of the hardest working people I meet. They're experts at what they do — whether that's running a contracting company, a medical practice, a retail shop, or a service business. What they often don't have is time to think strategically about their personal financial picture.

That's a gap worth closing. Because the financial decisions you make as a business owner — about retirement accounts, cash flow, and long-term planning — can have a significant impact on your future.

Key Financial Planning Areas for Small Business Owners

1 Retirement Planning — You Don't Have an Employer Doing It for You

One of the biggest differences between business owners and W-2 employees is that nobody is automatically setting aside retirement savings on your behalf. That means it's entirely up to you to build that structure. The good news is that business owners often have access to retirement plan options with higher contribution limits than a standard IRA — including SEP-IRAs, SIMPLE IRAs, and Solo 401(k)s. Understanding which option fits your business structure and income level is an important first step.

2 Separating Business and Personal Finances

Many small business owners — especially in the early years — blur the line between business and personal finances. Over time, this makes it harder to understand the true profitability of your business and to plan effectively for your personal financial future. Getting clarity here is foundational.

3 Planning for an Exit

Whether you plan to sell your business, pass it on to family, or simply wind it down someday, having a plan matters. The value of your business may be a significant part of your net worth — and relying on it entirely as your retirement plan carries real risk. Diversifying outside the business is worth thinking about early.

4 Protecting What You've Built

Business owners face unique risks — liability, loss of income if you're unable to work, and the impact of losing a key person in the business. Making sure your personal financial plan accounts for these risks is part of building a complete picture.

5 Cash Flow and Personal Income Planning

Business income can be irregular, seasonal, or unpredictable. Building a personal financial plan that accounts for that variability — rather than assuming a steady paycheck — is important for both day-to-day stability and long-term planning.
