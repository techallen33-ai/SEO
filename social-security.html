Social Security

Social Security Timing — When Should You Claim?

One of the most important retirement decisions you'll make — and one of the most misunderstood.

For most Americans, Social Security represents a meaningful — sometimes the largest — source of guaranteed retirement income. And yet the decision of when to claim is one that many people make without fully understanding the long-term implications.

There's no universally right answer. But there are important factors worth understanding before you decide.

The Basics: How Timing Affects Your Benefit

You can begin claiming Social Security retirement benefits as early as age 62. However, claiming before your Full Retirement Age (FRA) — which is 67 for most people born after 1960 — permanently reduces your monthly benefit. Every year you delay past your FRA increases your benefit by approximately 8%, up until age 70.

62 Earliest Eligibility

Permanently reduced monthly benefit. More checks over time, but each one is smaller.

67 Full Retirement Age*

Full benefit amount. The baseline for most people born after 1960.

70 Maximum Benefit

Delayed credits stop here. Waiting past 70 provides no additional increase.

*Full Retirement Age varies based on birth year. Check ssa.gov for your specific FRA.

Factors That Influence the Decision

1 Your Health and Life Expectancy

Social Security timing is, in part, a longevity question. If you claim early, you receive more checks — but smaller ones. If you delay, you receive fewer checks — but larger ones. The break-even point, where delayed claiming pays off, typically falls somewhere in your late 70s to early 80s. Your health and family history are worth factoring in.

2 Whether You're Still Working

If you claim Social Security before your Full Retirement Age and continue working, your benefits may be temporarily reduced depending on how much you earn. Once you reach FRA, this earnings limit no longer applies.

3 Your Other Sources of Retirement Income

If you have other income sources — a pension, retirement account withdrawals, or a spouse's income — you may have more flexibility to delay Social Security and let your benefit grow. If Social Security is your primary income source, the calculus may be different.

4 Spousal and Survivor Benefits

If you're married, the Social Security decision becomes more complex. Your claiming age affects not just your own benefit, but potentially your spouse's survivor benefit as well. For couples, coordinating claiming strategies can be an important part of retirement income planning.

5 The Tax Consideration

Depending on your total income in retirement, a portion of your Social Security benefit may be subject to federal income tax. This is worth understanding as part of your broader retirement income picture — though the specifics vary by situation and are worth discussing with a qualified tax professional.

There's No Perfect Formula — But There Is a Right Process

The Social Security decision is too important to make based on a rule of thumb or because a neighbor told you what they did. It's worth modeling out your specific situation — your benefit amount, your other income, your health, and your goals — before deciding.
