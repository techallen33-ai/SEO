Tax-Aware Planning

What to Consider Before a Roth Conversion

Roth conversions can be a meaningful part of a retirement strategy — but they're not right for everyone. Here's what to understand first.

What Is a Roth Conversion?

A Roth conversion is the process of moving money from a traditional IRA or 401(k) into a Roth IRA. The key difference between the two account types is how they're taxed:

Traditional IRA / 401(k)

Contributions are typically made pre-tax. You pay taxes when you withdraw the money in retirement.

Roth IRA

Contributions are made with after-tax dollars. Qualified withdrawals in retirement are generally tax-free.

When you convert, you're essentially paying taxes now in exchange for tax-free growth and withdrawals later. Whether that trade-off makes sense depends entirely on your individual situation.

Key Considerations Before Converting

1 Your Current vs. Future Tax Rate

The core question behind any Roth conversion is whether you expect your tax rate to be higher or lower in retirement than it is today. If you expect to be in a higher bracket later, converting now — and paying taxes at a lower rate — may make sense. If you expect your rate to drop in retirement, the calculus may be different.

2 Where Will You Get the Money to Pay the Taxes?

Converting triggers a taxable event. Ideally, you'd pay the resulting tax bill from outside funds — not from the converted amount itself. Using money from the converted account to cover taxes reduces the long-term benefit of the conversion.

3 Your Time Horizon

Roth conversions tend to make more sense the longer your time horizon. The tax-free growth has more time to compound, and you have more years to recover the upfront tax cost. Conversions closer to retirement may still make sense depending on your situation, but the math is worth examining carefully.

4 RMD Planning

Traditional IRAs and 401(k)s are subject to Required Minimum Distributions starting at a certain age. Roth IRAs are not subject to RMDs during the owner's lifetime. For people who don't need the income and want to reduce future RMDs, a conversion strategy may be worth exploring.

5 The Conversion Window

For some retirees, the years between leaving work and claiming Social Security can represent a period of temporarily lower taxable income. This window is sometimes used strategically to convert funds at a potentially lower tax rate. Whether this applies to your situation depends on your income sources and overall financial picture.

This Is a Planning Decision — Not a One-Size-Fits-All Answer

Roth conversions involve tax implications that vary significantly based on individual circumstances. This article is intended to be educational — not a recommendation to convert. Before making any decisions, it's worth having a conversation with a financial advisor and a qualified tax professional to evaluate what makes sense for your specific situation.
